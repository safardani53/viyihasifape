On patrol with the concerned residents walking Southamptons estates to clear them of violence and

Posted by Larita Shotwell on Wednesday, May 8, 2024

CLAD in a stab vest, burly Scott Healey greets the posse of concerned neighbours who have gathered in the dark outside Tesco.

They are sickened by crime on their streets and have been ­frustrated by overstretched ­policing — so they have taken matters into their own hands.

At around 9.30pm Scott signals the five-strong patrol, who have been recruited on Facebook, to move off with The Sun in tow.

Dressed in black, the 28-year-old doorman explains: “We patrol the streets making sure there’s no crime being committed.

“If there is, we try and deter. We’re not violent.

“We shouldn’t have to do this. It’s down to a lack of police resources.”

Torch in hand, Scott leads the group — a chatty middle-aged woman, a muscular lad in a baseball cap, a bearded man in his twenties and a young woman with a friendly Staffie cross.

The crew head through a rabbit warren of alleyways on a gritty estate on the fringes of Southampton.

They are among a 20-plus pool of volunteers which includes “former military, teachers, mums and elderly people”.

The members call themselves S019 Neighbourhood Watch after their Southampton suburb’s postcode. ­Others have called them vigilantes.

A YOUTH IS PERCHED ON A SMASHED PHONE BOX

Sholing and Bitterne — the neighbourhood patrolled by the group — saw 1,098 violent crimes and 385 cases of criminal damage and arson between June 2018 and July 2019.

Their Facebook page — liked by more than 3,000 people — says: “Getting annoyed with the youths of today thinking they can rob from hard-working people. Time to show some community spirit and catch the little salads (a local term for yobs).”

Scott — who patrols with “security” emblazoned on his back and a bike-locking chain strapped around his chest — insists: “A lot of people have said we’re vigilantes but we’re not.

“We’re there to observe and not get involved. Our protocol if we see something happening is to stand at a safe distance, get video footage, don’t get involved and call the police.”

The group was sparked into life around three weeks ago when there was an attempted break-in at an elderly woman’s home.

Former industrial cleaner Scott said: “Police didn’t want to know. They’re short-staffed.”

Earlier in the day I had seen a youth perched on top of a trashed phone box and smelt the whiff of cannabis near a fly-tipped bed.

But on this occasion, the time I spent patrolling with the group was mercifully free from crime or anti-social behaviour.

Volunteers split into teams to patrol the Thornhill, Sholing, Weston and Woolston areas of Southampton on foot or in cars every night.

The patrols offer an insight into a crime-plagued nation where the thin blue line has become thinner.

Since 2010, when there were just over 171,600 officers in the UK, police numbers have fallen annually.

By March this year there were just over 150,000 officers — a decrease of 12.4 per cent in nine years.

In Hampshire, where Southampton is the largest city, there were the equivalent of 2,835 full-time ­officers in March 2018 — a drop of 61 on 2017.

More than 600 police stations have closed across Britain in the past nine years. Cities the size of Bath and St Albans no longer have a dedicated cop shop.

In some towns, if you want to speak to an officer you have to call a freephone number.

Little wonder that recorded crime is at a 14-year high.

Last year violent offences soared by almost a fifth. Homicide rose to its highest level for nearly a decade.

But in the 12 months to March, just 7.8 per cent of recorded offences resulted in a criminal charge.

So it is hardly surprising unofficial citizen patrols have sprung up across Britain. In November The Sun visited ­Hartlepool. One Saturday, not a single officer was on duty in the town of 92,000, as all were on another job. A local group was set up to fill the gap.

Another team formed in Solihull, West Mids, where police credited them with forcing drug dealers away and reducing anti-social behaviour. ­Others patrol in Essex.

In Kidsgrove, Staffs, vigilantes  threatened to use baseball bats to target thugs in a crime-ridden park.

Some cops have warned against the rise in have-a-go-hero groups.

‘THEY BURNED TREE HOUSE AND SPRAYED PAINT’

Last year Inspector Iftekhar Ahmed, of West Midlands police, said he was concerned they were “hindering the situation”.

But amid the tower blocks and ­pebble-dashed estates of SO19, the patrol group seems popular. Retired electrician David Elliot, 69, told how 30 thugs spilled out from a house party last week.

They smashed up his fence to use as weapons and were said to have assaulted a 17-year-old girl then threatened her dad with an air gun.

The dad of three said: “I was ­terrified. I’ve never known anything like it. It was drunken hooligans.”

He supports the patrols, adding: “The police should be doing it but they’ve had their numbers cut. We’re like a Third World country.”

Thornhill resident Gemma Rowe said her 11-year-old autistic son Jay, who also has ADHD, was devastated after three thugs stole his new £600 mountain bike three weeks ago — scuppering plans to take him on a family cycling trip.

The full-time carer said: “There’s no police in the area. No one was helping us until the patrol started.”

Mum-of-two Jo Lamb said Thornhill Adventure Park, where she took her kids to play, was destroyed by teenagers a couple of weeks ago. The 38-year-old care worker from Sholing said: “They burned the tree house and sprayed paint everywhere. The swings have been ripped out.

“Crime like this is driving people back inside their houses.”

Jo said Scott’s SO19 group is “already making a difference”.

She added: “The areas they patrol have seen much less crime.

“But it’s not stopping it. The kids doing it just move to other areas. It’s terrible they’ve had to set up a patrol. Nobody should have to put themselves in danger like that.

“People on the street could be carrying weapons. It should be up to the police instead.”

THIN BLUE LINE AT 'BREAKING POINT'

The SO19 group recently met Hampshire Constabulary.

The force’s Superintendent Kelly ­Whiting said: “We promote active citizenship. However, we do not condone vigilantism. We believe the group understand this.

“We explained we have to prioritise offences that cause most harm.”

Sergeant Zoe Wakefield, of the Hampshire Police Federation, said the thin blue line is “definitely at breaking point in some areas”.

She admitted that officers are sometimes made to work enforced overtime to make up the shortfall. And she revealed hard-stretched officers failed to respond when her husband’s bike was stolen recently.

Sgt Wakefield added: “We have to prioritise crimes. Some things have to drop down the list.”

Prime Minister Boris Johnson promised last month to recruit more than 20,000 extra police ­officers within three years.

But cops point out the new ­officers will also need extra admin staff, equipment and vehicles.

Back in the dark alleyways of SO19, Scott welcomes the prospect of more cops — but until they appear he’ll be on the beat instead.

“We want more volunteers and more patrols,” he says.

“We just want people to feel safe on the streets and in their homes.”
